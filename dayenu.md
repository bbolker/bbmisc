## Updating the Haggadah

Here it is – another Passover, another year, another time to reflect and another frantic not-quite-enough-time-allotted-before-the-Seder to do it in, like not having enough time to prepare for class but finally, after years, beginning to accept that the Seder will go OK anyway. I'm sure the discussion will be fine, with all these opinionated and overeducated  people to join in: but what do I think we need to add to the Haggadah this year?

Really things don’t change: there is the French proverb (plus ça change, plus c'est la même chose), and I'm sure there’s a Yiddish one too. I don't think human nature changes, nor the possibilities for human hope and despair, nor the kindness or cruelty with which we treat our nearer and farther neighbors. Sure, things happened this year in our own lives and in our families' lives that we can celebrate, or can only celebrate surviving, and sure there is plenty to mourn and celebrate in our country and in the world: that's for the class to discuss. 

The world is fractal: as we look farther backward or forward in time, or farther inward or outward from ourselves, we can always find goodness that is better than the best we have seen and badness that is worse than the worst we have seen; at the same time, there can be moments and spots very close to us that are almost as extreme when put in our own personal perspectives, as the things that happen "out there". Seeing kids grow up healthy and happy, old friends sicken and die, friends and relatives make each other joyous and miserable, global climate change, war and peace in the Middle East, hundreds die from virus in Angola, millions not die from polio and smallpox ... like some probability distributions, the entire collection doesn’t have an average: there's always something huger and more diffuse or tinier but more connected tipping the scales in another direction.

In the midst of this confusion, I think the only conclusion I can reach is "dayenu": it would have been enough, it is enough, despite the unfairness (both in my favor and against me) of my life, I have to celebrate it. One of the reasons for Passover is to celebrate that we and the world made it (shehechyanu), yet again – and joined with that as always is the need to remember the dark side, the bitter herbs with the honey cake and macaroons. I have had a good year this year – but even if I had had a bad one, I hope I could still remember that what I have would have been enough.

---

Benjamin Bolker, 2005
